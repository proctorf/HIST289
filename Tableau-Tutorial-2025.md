# Tableau Tutorial: Historical Data Visualization

*A practical guide for creating compelling historical visualizations*

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this tutorial, you will be able to:
- Connect to historical datasets in Tableau
- Create basic charts and maps for historical analysis
- Build an interactive dashboard
- Share your work via Tableau Public

---

## Part 1: Getting Started

### Download Tableau Public (Free)
1. Go to https://public.tableau.com/
2. Click "Download Tableau Public"
3. Install and create a free account

### Your First Look at Tableau
When you open Tableau, you'll see:
- **Connect** (left): Where you'll load your data
- **Open** (center): Recent work and samples
- **Discover** (right): Examples for inspiration

---

## Part 2: Loading Your Data

### Connecting to a CSV File
1. Click **Text file** under "Connect"
2. Navigate to your data file and select it
3. Tableau will show you a preview of your data

### Understanding Your Data in Tableau
Once connected, you'll see:
- **Blue fields (Dimensions)**: Categories like names, dates, locations
- **Green fields (Measures)**: Numbers like population, casualties, amounts
- **Date fields**: Automatically detected dates with calendar icons

---

## Part 3: Creating Your First Chart

### Making a Simple Bar Chart
1. Click **Sheet 1** at the bottom to start visualizing
2. Drag a category field (blue) to **Rows**
3. Drag a number field (green) to **Columns**
4. Tableau automatically creates a bar chart!

### Essential Chart Types for Historical Data

#### Timeline/Line Chart
- Drag date field to **Columns**
- Drag number field to **Rows**
- Perfect for showing trends over time

#### Map
- Drag geographic fields (State, Country) to the canvas
- Tableau automatically creates a map
- Add numbers to **Size** or **Color** for deeper analysis

#### Scatterplot
- Drag one number to **Columns**
- Drag another number to **Rows**
- Great for comparing two variables

---

## Part 4: Making Charts More Meaningful

### Adding Detail with Color
- Drag a category field to **Color** on the Marks card
- Different categories get different colors automatically

### Sizing by Importance
- Drag a number field to **Size** on the Marks card
- Larger values = larger marks

### Filtering Your Data
- Drag any field to **Filters** shelf
- Choose which values to include/exclude
- Perfect for focusing on specific time periods or regions

---

## Part 5: Building a Dashboard

### Combining Multiple Charts
1. Create 2-3 different charts on separate worksheets
2. Click the **New Dashboard** tab (next to sheet tabs)
3. Drag your worksheets from the left panel onto the dashboard canvas

### Making Charts Interactive
1. Select a chart on your dashboard
2. Click the small dropdown arrow that appears
3. Choose **Use as Filter**
4. Now clicking on that chart will filter other charts!

---

## Part 6: Common Historical Visualizations

### Population Over Time
- **X-axis**: Date/Year
- **Y-axis**: Population count
- **Color**: Different regions or groups
- **Chart type**: Line chart

### Geographic Distribution
- **Base**: Map (drag geographic field to canvas)
- **Size**: Population or frequency
- **Color**: Categories or time periods

### Comparison Charts
- **X-axis**: Categories (states, battles, etc.)
- **Y-axis**: Numbers to compare
- **Color**: Sub-categories
- **Chart type**: Bar chart

### Before/After Analysis
- **Rows**: What you're measuring
- **Columns**: Time periods (before/after)
- **Color**: Categories
- **Chart type**: Side-by-side bars

---

## Part 7: Polishing Your Work

### Adding Titles and Labels
- Double-click any chart title to edit it
- Right-click axes to format labels
- Use clear, descriptive titles that explain what you're showing

### Choosing Good Colors
- Right-click the color legend
- Choose "Edit Colors"
- Pick colors that make sense (blue for water, green for growth, etc.)

### Adding Context
- Use **Text** objects to add explanations
- Include data sources and dates
- Explain what patterns the viewer should notice

## Tips for Success

### Start Simple
- Begin with basic charts before trying complex visualizations
- One clear message per chart is better than multiple confusing ones

### Know Your Data
- Understand what each column represents
- Check for missing or unusual values
- Consider the historical context of your data

### Design for Your Audience
- Use clear titles and labels
- Choose colors that support your message
- Test your charts with others to ensure clarity
